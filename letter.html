An open letter

Dear Andy Burnham,

An open letter to the Mayor of Greater Manchester and Member of Parliament for Makerfield — on the soft cage, the principle that unlocks it, and one reform that could change the country.

I am writing to you not as a party activist, and not as someone seeking office or favour, but as a citizen who has spent a long time trying to name what has gone wrong with the way Britain is governed — and who has become convinced that you, more than any other figure in our public life, already understand the answer in your bones.

Let me state the problem plainly, because I think you will recognise it. Britain has not been losing its freedom to some dramatic tyranny. It has been losing it to distance. Decade by decade, and under governments of every colour, the power that acts on people’s lives has drifted further and further from the people it acts upon. The bank that freezes an account without a reason; the algorithm that flags an innocent face; the magistrate who convicts by a letter that never arrived; the department that rations an operation from two hundred miles away — in every case, power and accountability have been allowed to live in different places. I have come to call the result the soft cage: not a prison, but a structure of polite, procedural, low-level supervision that no one ever voted for and that no one quite knows how to answer.

I have written a book about this — The Soft Cage — and the more I worked on it, the clearer one principle became. If the cage is built of distance, the key is proximity. The whole argument reduces to a single line: no power over a citizen without a face the citizen can reach. Power and accountability must share a postcode. And the instinct that follows is not the weary managerial one — manage the discontent, monitor the risk — but a builder’s instinct: build, don’t bully.

I am writing to you specifically because you have spent your time as Mayor of Greater Manchester demonstrating that this is not a theory but a practice. You took back control of the buses and ran them better. You built a politics whose mandate comes from a place rather than from patronage in a distant capital. And on at least one occasion the whole country remembers, you stood on the steps of your own city and refused to let the centre treat its people as a line in a spreadsheet. That was a place holding power to account — the very principle of this book, made real in a single afternoon. And now, as Member of Parliament for Makerfield while remaining Mayor of Greater Manchester, you stand in both of the worlds this letter is about at once: the distant centre that has drifted out of people’s reach, and the place that still answers for itself.

Move the centre.

I mean it literally. Take Parliament out of London and let it sit, on rotation, between Birmingham and Manchester — six months in each, in a new chamber built to house a different kind of politics altogether. The rotation is the heart of the geography: by refusing to crown a single new capital, the reform becomes a shared national project rather than a regional prize, binds the Midlands and the North into a common cause, and makes the point unmistakable — that political power belongs to the country, not to one square mile of it.

But the building itself is not a detail — it is the point. The book argues for a proportional voting system, one that ends the manufactured majorities of the present arrangement and produces a Parliament that is genuinely multi-vocal: many parties, real coalitions, a spectrum of opinion rather than two opposing blocs. You cannot house that politics in the chamber we have. Westminster’s geometry — two sets of benches facing each other at the length of two drawn swords — is the two-party system rendered in oak and stone, built for confrontation and quietly enforcing it. A multi-vocal, proportionally representative Parliament needs a multi-vocal setting: a chamber in the round, where members sit across a spectrum and face a common centre, where coalitions can form and negotiate in the open. It is no accident that the Scottish Parliament, the Senedd and most of Europe’s legislatures are built as hemicycles; they were designed for the plural politics we have so far refused ourselves.

And the cost, so often the first objection, is the weakest of all: a one-off outlay that amortises to a rounding error against the savings a capable state unlocks. Britain has already run a smaller version — the BBC’s move to Salford — and its honest, mixed result teaches the real lesson: move the whole ecosystem, not one institution, and give it time.

Moving the centre is the most visible part of a larger idea, which the book calls the Civic Capacity Covenant: that a government must build, protect its citizens from concentrated private power, represent the whole country, restrain itself under the rule of law, and leave people materially freer than it found them. It is neither left nor right. And it is costed to be funded largely by reallocation, not by money the country does not have.

I should be clear about what this letter is and is not. It is not an endorsement, and you have not asked for it; it is one citizen’s argument, offered in good faith. A renewal that depended on a single person would betray its own thesis — that these instincts must be built into the country’s structures so they outlast whoever holds office. But the spine of the argument — place before party, competence before spectacle, power made answerable to the people it acts upon — is yours already, more than anyone’s. That is why I am writing to you.

Britain does not need to abandon its traditions. It needs to stop confusing tradition with permanence. There are moments when politicians merely manage decline, and moments when they reshape the direction of a country. I believe we are at one of the latter — and that the argument finds its most natural advocate in you.

Yours sincerely,

Adam Heath

Author of The Soft Cage
