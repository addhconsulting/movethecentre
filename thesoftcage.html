The book

A short book on British freedom
THE SOFT CAGE

How Britain forgot it was free — and how it can remember

Adam Heath

The argument, in full.

The Soft Cage traces how a free people came to be quietly enclosed — in policing, the courts, speech, banking, the workplace, surveillance and the slow collapse of trust — and then turns, deliberately, toward renewal: an economics of capacity, a constitutional covenant, and the case for moving the centre of the country itself.

It is not a book of complaint. Each chapter carries a real-world example, because someone, somewhere, has already solved a version of each problem — from Vienna’s housing to Estonia’s data, Ireland’s citizens’ assemblies to Finland’s classrooms.

There is a particular quality to the light just before a country changes its mind. This is not a book of despair, nor a catalogue of grievances dressed as analysis. It is a diagnosis followed by a prescription, written in the belief that the second is the more important half.

How the book is built

  • Part OneThe Cage — how a free people was quietly enclosed
  • Part TwoThe Rebellion — how the country withdrew, and why
  • Part ThreeThe Way Out — economics, covenant, Parliament, renewal

Fifteen short chapters and an epilogue, with a costed appendix. The whole argument rests on three lines: no power over a citizen without a face they can reach; power and accountability must share a postcode; build, don’t bully.
