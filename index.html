An argument for renewing the British state

Move the centre.

Britain’s trouble is not too much government but power put in the wrong places — intrusive where it should be restrained, feeble where it should be capable, and almost always too far from the people it acts upon. This is a short, hopeful argument for bringing power back within reach — and, in the end, for moving the country’s centre itself.

The diagnosis

Control has been quietly substituting for capacity.

For decades, and under governments of every colour, the British state has compensated for what it can no longer do with more of what it can still impose. It cannot build enough homes, so it manages the resentment. It cannot deliver infrastructure affordably, so it cancels the ambition. It cannot regulate early, so it punishes after the fact. It cannot maintain trust, so it monitors instead.

The result is a kind of soft authoritarianism: not a prison, but a structure of polite, procedural supervision that no one voted for. And beneath its many forms lies a single fault — power and accountability have been allowed to live in different places. The bank that freezes an account, the algorithm that flags a face, the office that rations care from afar: in each, someone is acted upon by a power they cannot reach.

The principle

If the cage is built of distance, the key is proximity.

The principle

No power over a citizen without a face the citizen can reach.

The test

Power and accountability must share a postcode.

The rule

Build, don’t bully.

A state earns the right to act by what it builds for people, not by what it controls about them. The argument is neither to shrink the state nor simply to fund it, but to make it capable enough not to govern by fear, and restrained enough not to govern by control.

The constitution · the Civic Capacity Covenant

A state legitimate by what it builds.

The Covenant makes the state’s legitimacy depend on what it builds, not on what it controls. Its operating rule fits in a line: no new coercive capacity without civic capacity. The state may not acquire a new power to monitor, enforce or restrict unless it can show a matching gain in its ability to build, protect and serve. Control must be earned by competence, never substituted for it.

Five tests of a legitimate state

  1. i

    Build

    Homes, energy, transport, water, schools, hospitals, digital systems — the material foundations on which freedom actually rests.

  2. ii

    Protect

    From monopolies, rent extraction, unsafe systems and concentrated private power no citizen can refuse or reach.

  3. iii

    Represent

    Through proportional democracy, regional power, and institutions that live outside one corner of one city.

  4. iv

    Restrain itself

    Through the rule of law, speech protections, transparency, and hard limits on surveillance and coercion.

  5. v

    Leave citizens materially freer

    More secure in home, work and family life — and so more able to speak, move, organise and refuse.

A Civic Capacity Act would carry the principle into law on four pillars: a National Missions Lock giving long-horizon projects protected, multi-decade funding1; a Public Value Test requiring major contracts and public algorithms to add capacity, not private rent or needless surveillance; a Civic Capacity Dividend returning publicly-created uplift — land values around a new station, planning gains — to the place that created it2; and a Restraint Clause binding every new power of monitoring to sunset dates, independent audit and appeal3. Each already has a working precedent somewhere in the world.

The economy · Civic Capacity Economics

Anti-rentier, not anti-market.

  1. i

    Borrow for assets, not for drift

    Borrowing to postpone a reckoning is a liability. Borrowing to build something that pays the country back — energy, housing, transport — is not the same act.

  2. ii

    Reward production; discipline rent

    The enemy is not wealth but unearned power — the controlled bottleneck, the speculative land uplift, the monopoly price.

  3. iii

    Rebuild public bargaining power

    Open-book pricing, capped termination payments and real technical competence turn a captive buyer back into a capable one.

  4. iv

    Treat household security as infrastructure

    People living at the edge of panic do not make free citizens. Security is the material basis of the freedom to speak, move and refuse.

The programme is designed to be funded largely by reallocation — by buying competently, capturing public-created land value, and disciplining rents rather than taxing work. The figures below test the scale and direction of the argument; they are not a forecast.

A working example · Vienna

Vienna built household security — and freedom with it.

Vienna houses around sixty per cent of its people in social or subsidised homes4 — the city itself owns some 220,000 apartments, Europe’s largest municipal landlord. Because the public supply is so large, it disciplines the whole market, and rents across Vienna sit far below those of comparable capitals. The city treated housing not as charity but as the foundation of free citizenship — and a citizen secure in their home is freer to change jobs, speak their mind and refuse exploitation.

The reform · move the centre

Take Parliament out of London.

The most visible reform is also the most literal. Take Parliament out of London and let it sit, on rotation, between Birmingham and Manchester — six months in each, in a new chamber built to house a plural, proportional politics. The rotation is the heart of it: by refusing to crown a single new capital, the reform becomes a shared national project rather than a regional prize, and keeps the institution honest, because a Parliament that must pack up and arrive somewhere else twice a year cannot quietly re-grow an insular village around itself.

The chamber is not a detail. Westminster’s two facing benches are the two-party system rendered in oak and stone, built for confrontation. A genuinely multi-vocal Parliament — many parties, real coalitions — needs a chamber in the round, where members sit across a spectrum and face a common centre, as the Scottish Parliament, the Senedd and most of Europe’s legislatures already do.

The cost is the weakest objection of all.

On the book’s ready-reckoner9, a one-off relocation on the order of £5 billion, amortised over its life, falls to roughly £0.25 billion a year — set against procurement savings in the tens of billions, a rounding error in the national accounts. The return is regional capacity that compounds: London’s output per hour worked sits about a quarter above the UK average — 26.2% in the latest ONS figures8 — a measure of the latent output stranded everywhere else when money, talent and institutions all pool in one place.

A working example · the BBC moves north

The catalyst works — but only if you move the ecosystem.

From 2011 the BBC moved five national departments to MediaCityUK on the old Salford docks; ITV followed. The early promises — up to fifteen thousand jobs — were not met quickly: the Centre for Cities found the move brought only a few thousand genuinely new jobs to Greater Manchester, much of it work displaced from elsewhere5, and academic study found no significant effect on total employment, only a small lift to local wages6.

Yet the longer view is warmer. Over the following decade a real creative and digital cluster took root: by KPMG’s assessment, the sector’s employment in Salford grew well over a hundred per cent across the 2010s as ITV, technology firms and the University of Salford gathered around the anchor7. The lesson is the whole argument in miniature: move one institution and oversell it, and you will be disappointed; move it, build the ecosystem deliberately around it, and give it a decade, and you shift a region’s centre of gravity. A Parliament — with its departments, regulators and press moved with it — is a catalyst of an altogether greater order.
